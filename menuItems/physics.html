5. Momentum. The principle of conservation of momentum.

According to the second law of dynamics

a = F/m

We will now introduce an important physical quantity - momentum. It combines two quantities used to describe the motion of bodies: mass and velocity. momentum, like energy, is subject to the principle of its conservation, thanks to which it is easier to predict the results of many mechanical phenomena, including body collisions.

The momentum of the body is the product of the vectors of its mass and velocity \(\overrightarrow p = m \cdot \overrightarrow v\).
Note that according to the rules of multiplying a vector by a number, momentum is a vector with a direction and sense of the velocity vector, and the values of momentum and velocity satisfy the condition: \(m = m \cdot v\). The unit of momentum is \(1 kg \cdot \frac{m}{s}\).

Thermodynamics

The Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics. Temperature.

Revision of Previous Matter

Thermodynamics uses the concepts of heat and temperature. Already at the beginnning of the 19th century, it was known that these concepts are related to the structure of matter.

In the past, heat was thought to be a kind of substance that is stored in an object and can move between objects.
In 1840, Joule, Rumford and others discovered that heat is a form of energy.

Brown (1827): observation of chaotic movements of plant pollen in water.
Einstein (1905): Brownian motions are caused by collisions of pollen with water molecules - direct proof for the atomic structure of matter.

Two systems are in thermal contact if they can exchange energy without doing macroscopic work. This form of energy is called heat.

Heat and Thermal Contact
If systems in thermal contact do not exchange energy, then they are in thermal equilibrium.
We say that if two systems are in thermal equilibrium then they have the same temperature.

Thermal Balance


\[T_A = T_B\]
The Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics
If the two systems \(A\) and \(B\) are in thermal equilibrium with \(C\), then \(A\) is also in thermal equilibrium with \(B\).

Heat Capacity. Calorimetry.

Revision of Previous Matter
Heat is the amount of energy transferred from the environment to a thermodynamic system due to the difference in temperature between the system and its environment. Heat
1 kcal (calorie) - the amount of heat needed to raise the temperature of 1g of water from 14.5°C to 15.5°C.
A food calorie corresponds to 1kcal.		 1kcal
SI unit of heat - J (Joule)
\(1cal = 4.186 J\) (mechanical equivalent of heat)
The amount of heat absorbed by the system \(\Delta Q\) necessary to change the temperature of the system is proportional to this change in temperature \(\Delta T\). Heat Capacity \[\Delta Q = C \Delta T\] \[\Delta Q = \int_{T_p}^{T_k} C (T)dT \]
Heat capacity of a system is proportional to the amount and type of matter in the system.
If the amount of matter is expressed by mass (m), than heat capacity (\(C\)): \[ C = c \cdot m, \] where \(c\) is specific heat and we can say that the amout of heat released or supplied: \[ \Delta Q = c \cdot m \cdot \Delta T \] Whereas when the amount of matter is expressed by number of moles (n): \[ C = C_m \cdot n, \] where \(C_m\) is molar specific heat. Thread of Execution and the Call Stack

Javascript is not a asynchronous language, 
it is a synchronous language with asynchronous 
capabilities.
JavaScript is a single-threaded language.
Thread is a single sequential flow of 
control - a process of a program:
  Thread of execution:
  op1 -> op2 -> op3 -> op4 -> Call Stack
                           -> Memory Heap

The Call Stack keeps track of functions (a stack 
of functions) to be executed & manages execution 
contexts. The Stacks are LIFO (last in first out).

  Call Stack :  third()
                second()
                first()
                Global EC (execution context)

A Stack is a data structure.

Example of a simple call stack:
                  function first() {
                    console.log('first...');
                  };
                  function second() {
                    console.log('second...');
                  };
                  function third() {
                    console.log('third...');
                  };
                  first();
                  second();
                  third();

                  output: first...
                          second...
                          third... 

Example of a just a little more complex call stack:
                  function first() {
                    console.log('first...');
                    second();
                  };
                  function second() {
                    console.log('second...');
                    third();
                  };
                  function third() {
                    console.log('third');
                  };
                  first();

                  output: first... 
                          second... 
                          third...
