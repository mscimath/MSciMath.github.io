Normal distribution, Gaussian distribution - description

Normal Distribution, otherwise known as Gaussian Distribution, Gaussian Curve is the most important theoretical probability distribution in statistics and also the most intuitive statistical distribution. In brief words, it describes a situation in the world where most cases are close to the avarage result, and the more a given result deviates from the avarage, the less represented it is. Most cases are close to the avarage. The farther we move away from the average result, the fewer cases there are. This can be easily applied to real situations.

Example: Intelligence level. Most people have an average level of intelligence, \(IQ = 100\). Much fewer people have the level of intelligence equal to \(IQ = 80\) or \(IQ = 120\), and even less, \(IQ = 65\) or \(IQ = 135\). The name "normal distribution" refers to the situation of normality, that is, the case that is most frequently represented in society, the cases below the avarage level or above the avarage are much less numerous. The greater the deviation, the smaller the number of observations. The discussed situation is described by the Gaussian curve, a graphical representation of the normal distribution.

To understand the chart, two symbols need to be explained, \(\mu \) - means mean value, avarage value; \(\sigma \) - means standard deviation.

As can be seen, about \(68% \) of the observations are close to the mean, within one standard deviation from the mean (a measure of distance in the statistical language). As you move away from the mean, the Gaussian curve goes down. Within a distance of two standard deviations, there is as much as 95% of the observations. Extreme values (at the extremities of the Gaussian curve) are represented by a negligible percentage of observations.

In practice, these two measures, mean and standard deviation, are sufficient values to determine the normal distribution. It results from the formula for the normal distribution density function, the so-called Gaussian curve.

The formula for the normal distribution density function: \[f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}} \] where:
\(x\) - the result of the observation
\(\mu\) - the expected value of the variable
\(\sigma \) - standard deviation of the variable.

In order to determine the probability density in the normal distribution for a given result, the expected value and standard deviation for the variable and the given result should be substituted into the formula.
