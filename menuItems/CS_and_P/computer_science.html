TCP/IP & ISO/OSI Layered Models

Overview of communication process on the example of ISO/OSI and TCP/IP layered models.

Why do we need models?

Mutual communication of devices in a computer network consists of several stages and several elements. Each of them is equally important because the tasks necessary for proper communication are carried out on each of them. These stages are defined by so-called layered models. Each of you who has already met with layered models knows that understanding this topic is the foundation for gaining further knowledge and skills in the field of computer networks.

There are two layered models, the TCP/IP model and the ISO/OSI reference model. (((OSI - the Open System Interconnect Seven-Layer Model, ISO - International Standards Organization, TCP - Transmission Control Protocol, IP - Internet Protocol))) On the one hand, they are similar to each other, but on the other, each of the models is characterized by a slightly different approach to the way of communication. However, before we discuss both of these models and explain the differences between them, we will tell what for and why they are used at all, what is their role and what are the benefits of their use.

The division of the network communication process into individual layers brings many benefits, the most important of which are:

  • easier definition of the rules and principles of communication (these rules and principles are called communication protocols),
  • the ability of network devices and software from different manufacturers to interact with each other,
  • the possibility of easier understanding of the entire communication process,
  • the ability to manage the communication process.

Before the data from the source device are sent to the end device, they must go through a long way, during which they are first marked, tagged, described with specific information allowing for their identification, then they are sent between many intermediary devices until they reach the recipient, who then is to interpret the data.

If there was no model that divides communication into smaller, easer to understand and manage stages and defines the tasks that must be performed on individual layers, it would be difficult to properly manage network communication because the multitude of solutions and technologies would cause huge chaos, difficult to Control. Let's imagine a situation where such a division into layers does not exist. There are no rules describing communication, and each hardware and software manufacturer creates its own, independent system.

Obviously, within the solutions of one company, communication would run very efficiently and fast, but the solutions of two independent companies could be incompatible with one another. In fact, in practice, we use network hardware and software from different companies, and this is possible thankds to the division into individual layers, which contain rules ans tasks describing their operation. These rules and tasks are the same for everyone, but each company, each manufacturer of both hardware and software can implement them in their own way.

The best example to illustrate it are operating systems. Some users use Windows, some use one of the Linux distributions, and others use MacOP. Each one of these systems is different, each performs network tasks in a different way, but in the end, on each of these systems, the website or e-mail will look the same, or at least similar. Thus, the most important benefits of using layered models include:

  • managing the communication process in the network,
  • defining its rules and tasks,
  • the possibility of interoperability at the hardware and software level of network products from different manufacturers,
  • and checking the correctness of communication.
